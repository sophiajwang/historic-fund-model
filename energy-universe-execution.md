## Key Methodological Note: Defining "Energy"

Energy requires the most careful scoping of the three sectors. We need to decide what we mean.

**Our definition: Energy Technology & Clean Energy.** Companies building new energy generation, storage, transmission, or transportation technologies. This includes solar, wind, batteries, EVs, hydrogen, advanced nuclear, grid software, carbon capture, biofuels, and geothermal.

**Excluded from the primary universe:**
- Incumbent fossil fuel companies (ExxonMobil, Chevron, ConocoPhillips, etc.) — these are benchmarks, not comparables
- Traditional utilities (Duke, Southern, Dominion) — unless they are pure-play renewables
- Upstream oil & gas services (Halliburton, Schlumberger, Baker Hughes)
- Midstream / pipeline companies

**Rationale:** The LP deck comparison should be space vs. bio vs. energy *technology* — industries where venture capital and growth equity play a role and where the return profile involves technology risk. Including ExxonMobil in "energy" would make the comparison meaningless.

**However:** NextEra Energy is borderline. It's a utility, but it's the largest wind/solar operator in the US and is often included in clean energy indices. Decision: Include NextEra and similar renewable-heavy utilities, but tag them as "renewable utility" sub-sector so they can be separated in analysis.

**Scale note:** Energy sits between space and bio. Larger than space (~57), smaller than bio (~220-290). Expect ~100-180 companies. We can be more thorough than bio but not fully exhaustive like space. The ETF-plus-supplements approach applies.

## Stage 2: Screen Results

### Screening Notes Specific to Energy

1. **Lithium/uranium mining companies** (Albemarle, Livent, Uranium Energy Corp, Energy Fuels): These are raw material suppliers. Decision: INCLUDE if their primary market is energy technology (battery materials, nuclear fuel). Albemarle is borderline (lithium is majority of revenue but they also do catalysts). Uranium miners are included — uranium's only significant use is nuclear energy.

2. **Semiconductor companies** (ON Semi, Wolfspeed, Navitas, indie Semi): These enable solar/EV but are semiconductor companies first. Decision: EXCLUDE from primary universe — they're downstream enablers like Garmin is for space. Note in appendix as supply chain.

3. **Software-only companies** (Aurora Solar, Arcadia, Watershed): If they're pure software with no energy generation/storage/hardware, they're borderline. Decision: INCLUDE energy management/grid software (Stem, Arcadia). EXCLUDE pure carbon accounting/ESG reporting (Watershed). The test: does the software directly control or optimize energy assets?

4. **YieldCos and clean energy finance** (Hannon Armstrong, Clearway, TerraForm): These are financial vehicles that own clean energy assets. Decision: INCLUDE renewable YieldCos and asset owners as "renewable utility" sub-sector. They represent deployed capital returns. EXCLUDE pure financial intermediaries.

5. **Non-US-founded, US-listed:** SolarEdge (Israel), Polestar (Sweden), FREYR (Norway), Northvolt (Sweden), Wallbox (Spain), REE (Israel). Decision: SolarEdge is borderline — significant US operations, US-listed, often treated as a US clean energy company. INCLUDE with a flag. Others: EXCLUDE if no US HQ.

**Key analytical observations for cross-industry comparison:**

1. **Energy has TWO bust cycles** (cleantech 1.0 ~2011, SPAC era ~2022-23). Space has one (SPAC ~2022-23). Bio has none of comparable magnitude (individual failures but no sector-wide bust).

2. **Energy's failure rate in the SPAC cohort is the highest of all three sectors.** At least 7 EV SPACs went bankrupt within 3 years. Space had 2-3 SPAC failures. Bio had 1-2.

3. **Energy has one Tesla-scale outlier** (~$1T+ peak) comparable to SpaceX in space. Bio has no single company of that magnitude — its value creation is distributed across many large acquisitions.

4. **Energy's M&A exit market is much thinner than bio's.** There are fewer multi-billion-dollar acquisitions. This may be because energy companies need physical assets (factories, power plants) which are harder to acquire, vs. bio where you're acquiring IP and clinical data.

5. **Government funding plays a larger explicit role in energy** (DOE loans, IRA tax credits, ARPA-E) than in space (NASA contracts) or bio (NIH grants fund research but rarely companies directly). This matters for return attribution — some energy returns are effectively subsidized.
